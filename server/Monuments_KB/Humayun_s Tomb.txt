Humayun’s Tomb
🕰️ History

Humayun’s Tomb was built in 1570 by Haji Begum (also known as Hamida Banu Begum), the widow of Mughal Emperor Humayun.

It was the first garden-tomb in India and marked a major turning point in Mughal architecture. Persian architects were invited for its design, introducing grand symmetry and formal gardens to Indian monuments.

The tomb later inspired many Mughal structures — including the Taj Mahal. Today, it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of Delhi’s most important historical monuments.

📍 Location

Humayun’s Tomb is located in New Delhi, near the Yamuna River, in a large landscaped complex surrounded by smaller tombs and gardens.

🏛️ Architecture

Built mainly with red sandstone and white marble

Designed in classic Mughal style with Persian influence

Set in a Charbagh garden (four-part garden symbolizing paradise)

Large central dome surrounded by smaller domes

High arched entrances on all sides

Raised platform elevates the main tomb structure

Inside lies Humayun’s cenotaph, while the actual grave is below

Key architectural features include symmetry, geometric planning, and balanced proportions — elements that became standard in later Mughal monuments.

✨ Interesting Facts

It is the first monumental Mughal tomb in India.

More than 150 Mughal family members are buried in the complex.

Served as a refuge for Mughal royalty during British rule.

Often called the architectural predecessor of the Taj Mahal.

The gardens were restored using traditional Mughal layouts.
