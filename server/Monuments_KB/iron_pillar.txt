Iron Pillar of Delhi
🕰️ History

The Iron Pillar dates back to around the 4th century CE, during the Gupta period. An inscription on the pillar mentions a king named Chandra, who is widely identified as Chandragupta II.

Originally, the pillar is believed to have stood at another site (possibly in central India) and was later moved to Delhi. Today, it stands within the Qutub complex, representing the advanced metallurgical knowledge of ancient India.

The pillar is especially famous because it has resisted rust for over 1,600 years, despite being exposed to rain and changing weather — a remarkable scientific achievement.

📍 Location

The Iron Pillar is located inside the Qutub Complex in New Delhi, India.

🏛️ Architecture / Structure

Height: about 7.2 meters (24 feet)

Weight: approximately 6 tons

Made of nearly pure wrought iron

Smooth cylindrical shaft with a decorative capital on top

Sanskrit inscription carved in Brahmi script praising King Chandra

Unlike modern iron structures, it was forged without welding, showing extraordinary craftsmanship.

✨ Interesting Facts

The pillar has not rusted significantly for more than 16 centuries.

Scientists believe its corrosion resistance is due to a protective phosphorus-rich layer formed naturally over time.

Earlier, visitors believed that hugging the pillar with arms joined behind it brought good luck (this is now restricted to protect the monument).

It is considered one of the finest examples of ancient Indian metallurgy.

The pillar predates most large iron structures in the world.

🧭 Visiting Info

Open: Daily, sunrise to sunset

Entry: Included with Qutub Complex ticket

Best time to visit: Morning or late afternoon

Best season: October to March

Easily reachable by metro and local transport
